I am an independent researcher and development cooperation practitioner. My work sits at the intersection of foreign aid, democratic participation, and digital governance.

With a long-standing interest in democratic transition, I have spent the past three years in Tunisia, where I planned and managed Korea's bilateral aid programs with a focus on governance. A major part of this work has been expanding an e-governance portfolio that enhances citizen participation as a key path toward more transparent and accountable public institutions. Through this experience, I supported the development and approval of governance programs totalling about US$15 million.

I planned and led more than eight field study missions in development contexts where reliable data were often limited. I learned how to collect core data and information through semi-structured interviews, stakeholder consultations and site observations. I engaged with various stakeholders, ranging from senior government officials and technical experts to civil society actors. Focus group discussions with direct beneficiaries were especially important, as they provided insights often missing from formal interactions with government officials.

At the same time, my field experience allowed me to think over the limits of foreign aid. Many traditional donors are narrowing their agendas around national interest, while development finance is becoming more constrained. Democratic backsliding has accelerated in many transitioning countries, and autocrats are returning to power. In this space, authoritarian donors have expanded their influence by offering low-barrier forms of cooperation.

In countries where democratic institutions and procedures remain weak, artificial intelligence raises another concern. Digital systems can support citizen participation, but they can also become tools of public surveillance and control.

These concerns now shape the questions I want to address. I am interested in the relationship between technology and democratic participation in development contexts. This is closely linked to the question of how international donors can support this objective under tighter financial constraints. Another part of my work looks at how authoritarian donors expand their influence, and how this affects democratic institutions in transitioning countries.
